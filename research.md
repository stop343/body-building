# Nutrition & Supplements for Muscle Growth During Bulking

> Evidence-based guide — only supplements with published research, low risk profiles,
> and realistic expectations are included. This is **not** a replacement for medical advice.

---

## Nutritional Foundation (Before Any Supplement)

Supplements are **supplementary**. Without these fundamentals in place, no supplement will produce meaningful results.

### Caloric Surplus

| Parameter | Recommendation | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Daily surplus | **+200 to +400 kcal** above maintenance | Systematic reviews on body composition |
| Rate of weight gain | **0.25–0.5% of body weight per week** | Minimizes fat accumulation |
| Beginner adjustment | Can tolerate upper end (~400–500 kcal) | Higher responsiveness to training stimulus |
| Advanced adjustment | Lower end (~150–300 kcal) | Closer to genetic ceiling, fat gain risk increases |

> Exceeding ~500 kcal surplus does **not** proportionally increase muscle synthesis — it primarily increases fat storage because the body has a physiological limit on how fast it can build new muscle tissue.

### Protein Intake

| Parameter | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Daily protein | **1.6–2.2 g/kg body weight** |
| Per-meal leucine threshold | **2–3 g leucine per meal** (triggers MPS) |
| Meals per day | 3–5 protein-containing meals, evenly distributed |
| Diminishing returns | Benefits plateau above ~2.0 g/kg/day for most individuals |

**Key paper:** Morton RW et al. (2018). *"A systematic review, meta-analysis and meta-regression of the effect of protein supplementation on resistance training-induced gains in muscle mass and strength in healthy adults."* — British Journal of Sports Medicine, 52(6), 376–384. (Meta-analysis of 49 studies, 1863 participants)

## Tier 1 — Strongly Evidence-Based Supplements

These have the most robust research support, excellent safety profiles, and deliver measurable, realistic results.

---

### 1. Creatine Monohydrate

**What it does:** Increases phosphocreatine stores in muscle → enhances ATP regeneration → allows higher training volume, intensity, and quality during resistance training.

| Detail | Information |
|---|---|
| **Dosage** | 3–5 g/day (no loading phase necessary) |
| **Timing** | Any time of day; consistency matters more than timing |
| **Form** | Creatine monohydrate specifically (most studied form) |
| **Safety** | Extensively studied; no adverse effects in healthy individuals at recommended doses. ISSN position stand confirms safety. |
| **Cost** | Very low (~$0.05–0.10/day) |

**Realistic expectation:**

- **+5–10% improvement** in strength and power output over 8–12 weeks
- **+1–2 kg lean body mass** over 8–12 weeks (partly from intracellular water retention + actual contractile tissue)
- Acts as a **"volume amplifier"** — you can do more work per session, which drives more hypertrophy over time

**What it will NOT do:**

- It will not build muscle on its own without resistance training
- Initial weight gain (1–2 kg in the first week) is water, not muscle

**Key papers:**

- Kreider RB et al. (2017). *"International Society of Sports Nutrition position stand: safety and efficacy of creatine supplementation."* — Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition, 14, 18.
- Lanhers C et al. (2017). *"Creatine Supplementation and Upper Limb Strength Performance: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis."* — Sports Medicine, 47(1), 163–173.
- Rawson ES & Volek JS (2003). *"Effects of creatine supplementation and resistance training on muscle strength and weightlifting performance."* — Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research, 17(4), 822–831.

---

### 2. Whey Protein (or equivalent high-quality protein supplement)

**What it does:** Provides essential amino acids (EAAs) — particularly leucine — to stimulate muscle protein synthesis (MPS) via the mTOR pathway. Most useful when dietary protein is inadequate.

| Detail | Information |
|---|---|
| **Dosage** | 20–40 g per serving (to hit leucine threshold) |
| **Timing** | Within the broader daily distribution; per-meal protein matters more than the "anabolic window" |
| **Types** | Whey concentrate, whey isolate, or plant-based blends (with leucine ≥2 g) |
| **Safety** | Very safe for healthy individuals; well-tolerated. Use isolate if lactose-intolerant. |
| **Cost** | Moderate (~$0.50–1.50/serving) |

**Realistic expectation:**

- Meaningful benefit **only if total daily protein is below ~1.6 g/kg/day** from whole foods
- If you already eat enough protein from food, adding a shake will provide **minimal additional hypertrophy benefit**
- Convenience tool — makes it easier to hit protein targets

**What it will NOT do:**

- It will not outperform whole food protein of equivalent amino acid profile
- Diminishing returns above ~2.0 g/kg/day total protein

**Key papers:**

- Morton RW et al. (2018). *"A systematic review, meta-analysis and meta-regression of the effect of protein supplementation on resistance training-induced gains in muscle mass and strength."* — BJSM, 52(6), 376–384.
- Schoenfeld BJ & Aragon AA (2018). *"How much protein can the body use in a single meal for muscle-building?"* — JISSN, 15, 10.
- Jäger R et al. (2017). *"International Society of Sports Nutrition Position Stand: protein and exercise."* — JISSN, 14, 20.

---

### 3. Caffeine

**What it does:** Adenosine receptor antagonist → reduces perceived exertion, improves neural drive, increases power output, movement velocity, and muscular endurance during training.

| Detail | Information |
|---|---|
| **Dosage** | **3–6 mg/kg body weight** (~200–400 mg for most people) |
| **Timing** | **60 minutes** before training for peak plasma concentration |
| **Form** | Coffee, caffeine pills, or pre-workout (all work) |
| **Safety** | Safe at recommended doses. Side effects (jitters, sleep disruption, GI distress) increase above 6 mg/kg. |
| **Cost** | Very low |

**Realistic expectation:**

- **Acute performance improvement** in power, velocity, and reps-to-failure
- Stronger effect in **non-habitual users** — regular high-dose consumers see blunted response
- Indirectly supports hypertrophy by enabling **higher quality training sessions**

**What it will NOT do:**

- It does not directly build muscle tissue
- Chronic tolerance reduces the performance benefit
- Disrupts sleep if taken too late — and **sleep is critical** for recovery and growth

**Key papers:**

- Grgic J et al. (2020). *"Wake up and smell the coffee: caffeine supplementation and exercise performance."* — British Journal of Sports Medicine, 54(11), 681–688.
- Grgic J et al. (2018). *"Effects of caffeine intake on muscle strength and power: a systematic review and meta-analysis."* — JISSN, 15, 11.
- Goldstein ER et al. (2010). *"International Society of Sports Nutrition position stand: caffeine and performance."* — JISSN, 7(1), 5.

## Tier 2 — Conditionally Useful Supplements

Supported by research but with more conditional or modest effects. Worth considering if Tier 1 and nutritional fundamentals are already optimized.

---

### 4. Beta-Alanine

**What it does:** Increases intramuscular carnosine levels → buffers hydrogen ion accumulation → delays fatigue during high-intensity exercise lasting **1–4 minutes** (e.g., high-rep sets, supersets).

| Detail | Information |
|---|---|
| **Dosage** | **3.2–6.4 g/day**, split into smaller doses (0.8–1.6 g each) |
| **Timing** | Daily (chronic supplementation); takes 2–4 weeks to saturate |
| **Safety** | Safe. Common side effect: **paresthesia** (tingling skin) — harmless and dose-dependent |
| **Cost** | Low |

**Realistic expectation:**

- Improves **muscular endurance** in the 60–240 second effort range
- May allow **1–2 extra reps** on higher rep sets (10+ reps)
- **Does NOT directly increase strength or muscle mass** — helps indirectly by allowing more training volume

**What it will NOT do:**

- Minimal benefit for low-rep, heavy strength work (1–5 reps)
- Does not enhance 1RM

**Key paper:**

- Saunders B et al. (2017). *"β-Alanine supplementation to improve exercise capacity and performance: a systematic review and meta-analysis."* — British Journal of Sports Medicine, 51(8), 658–669. (Meta-analysis of 40 studies)

---

### 5. Vitamin D (if deficient)

**What it does:** Functions as a hormone affecting muscle fiber function, protein synthesis, calcium handling, and immune function. Deficiency impairs muscle performance and recovery.

| Detail | Information |
|---|---|
| **Dosage** | **1,000–4,000 IU/day** (dose depends on blood levels) |
| **Prerequisite** | **Get blood levels tested first** (25-hydroxyvitamin D) |
| **Target level** | 30–50 ng/mL (75–125 nmol/L) |
| **Safety** | Safe within recommended range. Toxicity possible at very high chronic doses (>10,000 IU/day without monitoring). |
| **Who needs it** | People with limited sun exposure, dark skin, or living at high latitudes |

**Realistic expectation:**

- If deficient: correcting levels can **restore** normal muscle function and strength
- If already sufficient (>30 ng/mL): **no additional benefit** from supplementation
- Does NOT supercharge muscle growth above normal physiology

**Key papers:**

- Chiang CM et al. (2017). *"Effects of Vitamin D Supplementation on Muscle Strength in Athletes: A Systematic Review."* — Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research, 31(2), 566–574.
- Tomlinson PB et al. (2015). *"Effects of vitamin D supplementation on upper and lower body muscle strength levels in healthy individuals."* — Journal of Science and Medicine in Sport, 18(5), 575–580.

---

### 6. Omega-3 Fatty Acids (Fish Oil)

**What it does:** EPA and DHA reduce chronic inflammation, may enhance muscle protein synthesis response to amino acids, and support recovery from training-induced damage.

| Detail | Information |
|---|---|
| **Dosage** | **2–3 g combined EPA+DHA per day** |
| **Form** | Fish oil capsules or liquid; look for high EPA+DHA concentration |
| **Safety** | Very safe. Mild GI effects at high doses. May interact with blood thinners. |
| **Cost** | Moderate |

**Realistic expectation:**

- May **modestly enhance MPS** when combined with protein intake
- Primary benefit is **recovery and inflammation management** — especially useful during high-volume training phases
- Effects are **subtle and long-term**, not acutely noticeable

**What it will NOT do:**

- Not a direct muscle-building supplement
- Effects are very modest compared to Tier 1 supplements

**Key papers:**

- McGlory C et al. (2016). *"Omega-3 fatty acid supplementation attenuates skeletal muscle disuse atrophy during two weeks of unilateral leg immobilization."* — American Journal of Clinical Nutrition, 103(1), 115–124.
- Smith GI et al. (2011). *"Omega-3 polyunsaturated fatty acids augment the muscle protein anabolic response to amino acids in healthy young and middle-aged men and women."* — Clinical Science, 121(6), 267–278.

## Supplements NOT Recommended (Insufficient Evidence or Overhyped)

| Supplement | Why NOT |
|---|---|
| **BCAAs** | Redundant if protein intake is adequate. Whey already contains all BCAAs. Meta-analyses show no added benefit beyond whole protein. |
| **Testosterone Boosters** (Tribulus, D-Aspartic Acid, etc.) | No reliable evidence for meaningful testosterone increase in healthy males. Overhyped marketing. |
| **HMB** | May help untrained beginners or during extreme caloric deficit. Neutral effect in trained individuals meeting protein needs. |
| **Glutamine** | Adequate amounts already present in dietary protein. No additional hypertrophy benefit in well-fed individuals. |
| **CLA** | Inconsistent evidence. Minimal practical effect on body composition. |
| **ZMA** | Only beneficial if zinc/magnesium deficient. No testosterone boost in non-deficient individuals (Wilborn et al., 2004). |

## The 80/20 Rule

The **vast majority** of your muscle growth results (~80%) will come from:

1. **Progressive resistance training** (the stimulus)
2. **Caloric surplus** of 200–400 kcal/day (the energy)
3. **Protein intake** of 1.6–2.2 g/kg/day (the building blocks)
4. **Sleep** of 7–9 hours per night (the recovery)

Supplements account for roughly **~5% of results at best**. Creatine is the one exception that comes closest to being genuinely impactful — and even then, it works by amplifying your training, not replacing it.
